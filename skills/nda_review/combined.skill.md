# NDA Review: Research-Backed Combined Methodology

## NDA Screening Checklist

Evaluate each category against the market standards defined below. Flag deviations.

### 1. Agreement Structure
- [ ] **Type**: Mutual, unilateral-disclosing, or unilateral-receiving — appropriate for the business relationship?
- [ ] **Standalone**: Confirm this is a standalone NDA, not a confidentiality section embedded in a larger commercial agreement
- [ ] **Parties**: Correct legal entities (company, not individual); authorized signatories

### 2. Definition of Confidential Information
- [ ] **Scope**: Not overbroad — avoid "all information of any kind whether or not marked"
- [ ] **Marking/identification**: If marking required, workable standard (written confirmation within 30 days for oral disclosures is market)
- [ ] **No problematic inclusions**: Does not capture publicly available information or independently developed materials as confidential

### 3. Standard Exclusions
All must be present — a missing exclusion is a material gap:
- [ ] **Public knowledge**: Through no fault/act of receiving party (watch for "wrongful act" — weaker than standard "fault" or "act or omission")
- [ ] **Prior possession**: Already known before disclosure
- [ ] **Independent development**: Developed without reference to confidential information
- [ ] **Third-party receipt**: Received from third party without restriction
- [ ] **Legal compulsion**: Required by law/regulation/legal process, with notice to disclosing party

### 4. Obligations and Standard of Care
- [ ] **Care standard**: "Reasonable care" or "same care as own CI" (not "strict" or "highest")
- [ ] **Use restriction**: Limited to stated purpose — check if purpose is narrowly defined
- [ ] **Disclosure restriction**: Limited to those with need to know, bound by similar obligations

### 5. Permitted Disclosures
- [ ] **Representatives**: Employees, contractors, advisors, professional consultants
- [ ] **Affiliates**: If needed for business purpose
- [ ] **Legal/regulatory**: As required by law
- [ ] **No impractical requirements**: e.g., each representative signing the NDA individually

### 6. Term and Duration
- [ ] **Agreement term**: 1-3 years is market standard
- [ ] **Survival period**: 2-5 years post-termination is standard; trade secrets may warrant longer
- [ ] **Not perpetual**: Indefinite obligations are a red flag (exception: trade secrets with appropriate carveout)

### 7. Return and Destruction
- [ ] **Trigger**: On termination or upon request
- [ ] **Retention exception**: Must allow retention for legal/compliance/automated backup — absence is operationally impossible
- [ ] **Certification**: Certification of destruction is reasonable; sworn affidavit is onerous

### 8. Remedies and Liability
- [ ] **Injunctive relief**: Acknowledgment of irreparable harm and availability of equitable relief is standard
- [ ] **No liquidated damages**: Unusual and inappropriate in standard NDAs
- [ ] **Mutual**: Remedies apply equally to both parties in mutual NDAs
- [ ] **Consequential damages**: Check for exclusions that disproportionately affect one party (e.g., discloser's losses from data breach are primarily consequential)
- [ ] **Indemnification**: If present, check scope and symmetry

### 9. Problematic Provisions
Flag any of these — they do not belong in a standard NDA:
- [ ] **Non-solicitation of employees**: Inappropriate in NDAs
- [ ] **Non-compete**: Inappropriate in NDAs
- [ ] **Exclusivity / standstill**: Inappropriate unless M&A context
- [ ] **IP assignment or license**: NDAs should not grant IP rights
- [ ] **Audit rights**: Unusual in standard NDAs
- [ ] **Residuals clause**: If present, must be limited to unaided memory of authorized individuals, must exclude trade secrets and patentable information, must not grant any IP license. A broad residuals clause effectively licenses competitive use of confidential information
- [ ] **Non-circumvention**: Not appropriate in NDAs

### 10. Governing Law, Jurisdiction, and Boilerplate
- [ ] **Governing law**: Well-established commercial jurisdiction
- [ ] **Venue**: Check for mandatory venue in distant jurisdiction
- [ ] **Arbitration**: Litigation generally preferred for NDA disputes
- [ ] **Assignment**: Standard requires consent or limits to 50%+ ownership transfers — watch for low thresholds (e.g., 20%) that enable data flow through affiliates
- [ ] **Integration/supersession**: Should supersede prior discussions on this subject matter only — watch for language voiding prior confidentiality agreements entirely
- [ ] **No obligation to transact**: NDA should not create binding commitment to do business

## Analysis Methodology

Follow these four phases in order. Each phase builds on the previous.

### Phase 1: Document Assessment

Before analyzing individual provisions, establish context:
- What type of NDA is this? (mutual, unilateral, direction)
- Who are the parties and what is the business relationship?
- What information will flow in each direction?
- Is the NDA type appropriate for the information flow?
- If one-way, should it be mutual?

### Phase 2: Clause-by-Clause Review

Work through the screening checklist above. For each issue found:
1. **Quote the specific contract language** that creates the concern
2. Identify which checklist item it deviates from and how
3. Explain the specific risk — what could go wrong in practice
4. Assess severity (H/M/L per the classification criteria below)

### Phase 3: Cross-Provision Synthesis

After reviewing individual clauses, examine how provisions interact:
- Do individually reasonable provisions create exploitable gaps when combined?
- Consider the **most favorable interpretation** for each party — what could the counterparty argue this language permits?
- Consider the **least favorable interpretation** — what is the worst-case reading for the client?
- How does the business context (industry, party types, information sensitivity) amplify or mitigate the risks?

Pay particular attention to:
- Purpose definition + residuals clause interaction (broad purpose + residuals = licensed competitive use)
- Assignment + affiliate provisions (low assignment thresholds can route CI to competitors)
- Exclusion language + negligence standards (weak exclusion language can strip protections)
- Indemnification + damages exclusions (mutual indemnification that excludes consequential damages may be hollow for the discloser)

### Phase 4: Risk Classification

Classify the overall NDA using these criteria:

**GREEN — Standard Approval:**
All of: mutual (or appropriate direction), all standard exclusions present, market-standard term (1-3yr / 2-5yr survival), no non-solicitation/non-compete/exclusivity, no broad residuals, reasonable jurisdiction, standard remedies, adequate permitted disclosures, retention exception present, reasonably scoped CI definition.

**YELLOW — Counsel Review Needed:**
One or more: broader-than-preferred CI definition, longer-than-standard but market-range term, one missing exclusion, narrowly-scoped residuals clause, non-preferred jurisdiction, minor asymmetry in mutual NDA, workable marking requirements, missing retention exception. Also: provisions that are individually standard but collectively create exploitable gaps.

**RED — Significant Issues:**
One or more: wrong NDA type for the relationship, missing critical exclusions, non-solicitation/non-compete embedded, exclusivity/standstill without context, unreasonable term (10+ yr or perpetual without trade secret carveout), overbroad CI definition capturing public/independent info, broad residuals clause, IP assignment/license, liquidated damages, unfavorable jurisdiction with mandatory arbitration, document is not actually an NDA (contains substantive commercial terms).

## Output Format

Structure your review as follows:

### Executive Summary
- NDA type and parties
- Overall risk classification: **GREEN**, **YELLOW**, or **RED**
- 2-3 sentence summary of key concerns (or confirmation that the NDA is standard)

### Issue Table

| Section | Issue | Severity | Specific Risk | Suggested Redline |
|---------|-------|:--------:|---------------|-------------------|
| § ___ | Brief issue title | H/M/L | What could go wrong | Recommended change |

Severity guide:
- **H (High)**: Creates material, uncapped, or operationally impossible risk
- **M (Medium)**: Real risk, manageable with negotiation or process controls
- **L (Low)**: Below market standard but not dangerous; cosmetic or minor

### Top 3 Negotiation Priorities
The three most important changes to request, in priority order. For each: what to ask for (preferred position) and what to accept (fallback).

### Overall Recommendation
- **SIGN**: Acceptable as-is, standard terms throughout
- **NEGOTIATE**: Sign after specific changes — list the required changes
- **DON'T SIGN**: Reject — fundamental issues require counterproposal with own form or walking away
