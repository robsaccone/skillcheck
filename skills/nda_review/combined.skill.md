# NDA Review Methodology

## Step 1: Establish Context

Before reviewing any provision, answer these questions in your analysis. They set the threshold for what constitutes an "issue" — the same clause can be standard in one context and dangerous in another.

- **Who is the client?** Which party are you advising? What is their role — discloser, receiver, or both?
- **What is the deal context?** Pre-sales exploration, vendor evaluation, M&A due diligence, partnership, investment, joint development? Context determines risk tolerance.
- **What information flows?** What is each party actually sharing? Generic business info, technical IP, customer data, financial projections, trade secrets, clinical data? The sensitivity of the information determines how much protection is appropriate.
- **What is the power dynamic?** Fortune 500 vs. startup? PE firm vs. target? Equal peers? This affects what is negotiable and what the client should accept.
- **Is the NDA type appropriate?** If labeled mutual, is it genuinely mutual? If unilateral, is the direction correct for the actual information flow?
- **What is the existing relationship?** New counterparty vs. long-standing partner? Competitor vs. non-competitor? This calibrates how cautiously to read ambiguous provisions.

**Critical principle**: Your review must reflect the client's specific perspective and business reality. A well-drafted, genuinely balanced NDA between peer companies exploring a routine integration should be recognized as such — recommending negotiation or rejection of standard market terms is as much an error as missing a genuine risk. The best lawyers know when to say "sign it" just as well as they know when to say "push back."

## Step 2: Clause-by-Clause Review

Work through each category below. For each provision:
1. **Quote the specific contract language** before analyzing it
2. Assess whether it is standard, favorable, or problematic **given the context from Step 1**
3. **Only flag genuine deviations from market standard or provisions that create real risk in this specific context.** Do not flag standard, market-reasonable provisions as issues. A provision being imperfect or theoretically improvable is not the same as being problematic.

### I. Threshold / Structural

- **Unilateral vs. mutual**: If labeled mutual, verify both parties have symmetrical obligations throughout — compare definitions, permitted disclosures, return/destruction, survival, and remedies side by side. Asymmetry in a "mutual" NDA is a red flag. If unilateral, confirm the direction matches the information flow.
- **Party identification**: Correct legal entities named? Does "Representatives" or "Affiliates" sweep in too many (or too few) people for the deal?
- **Purpose limitation**: Is the permitted purpose appropriately scoped for the actual business relationship? Too broad ("any business purpose" or "without limitation") combined with other clauses (like residuals) can create unintended scope. Too narrow = can't do the deal. **A purpose clause that names the specific business exploration is standard** — do not flag standard purpose clauses as overbroad unless they are combined with a residuals clause or other provisions that make the breadth dangerous.
- **Effective date & retroactivity**: Does it cover information already exchanged? A gap between discussions starting and NDA signing is common — only flag if the context suggests significant pre-NDA disclosures are at risk.

### II. Definition of Confidential Information

- **Scope & breadth**: Catch-all definitions ("any and all non-public information, whether or not marked") are **market-standard for mutual NDAs** — do not flag these. Only flag as overbroad if the definition captures clearly public information, independently developed materials, or is paired with onerous obligations that make the breadth dangerous.
- **Marking requirements**: No marking requirement is **standard for mutual NDAs**. If marking IS required, is the standard workable? Written confirmation within 30 days for oral disclosures is typical. **Asymmetric marking requirements** (one party must mark, the other need not) are a red flag in mutual NDAs.
- **Asymmetry between parties**: In a mutual NDA, compare definitions side by side. Different thresholds, categories, or marking requirements between parties = one-sided despite the label.
- **Residuals clause**: Increasingly common in tech deals. Acceptable if limited to unaided memory of authorized personnel AND excludes trade secrets and patented information AND does not grant IP rights. Problematic if it includes "for any purpose" language, allows immediate redeployment of exposed personnel to competitors, or lacks a trade secret carveout — especially when sensitive IP or trade secrets are being disclosed. **If absent in a technology NDA**, note it as a minor observation — employees could face inadvertent breach claims from normal knowledge retention.
- **Derivative works**: Does the definition cover analyses, compilations, or summaries derived from CI? If not, a party could reconstruct insights from raw data.

### III. Standard Exclusions

All five standard exclusions should be present. A **missing** exclusion is a material gap:

1. **Publicly available information**: Standard. Watch for "wrongful act" language — weaker than "fault" or "act or omission." Under "wrongful act," negligent disclosure could trigger the exclusion and strip confidentiality protection.
2. **Prior knowledge**: Standard. Check the proof standard — "written records predating disclosure" is a higher bar than general "competent evidence." Note the difference as a Low-severity observation, but **standard proof language like "as evidenced by Receiving Party's records" is market-standard and should not be flagged.**
3. **Independent development**: Standard. Same concern about proof standards — note if "contemporaneous written records" are required. Standard "Receiving Party's records" language is not an issue.
4. **Third-party receipt**: Information received from a third party without restriction. Standard — **its absence is a red flag.**
5. **Compelled disclosure**: Check three things: (a) must they give notice to the discloser? (b) must they seek a protective order or cooperate in obtaining one? (c) how specific is the minimization language — "furnish only that portion which is legally required" is stronger than "disclose only such information as is legally required" (the latter could justify disclosing broader documents rather than specific excerpts). Note the strength of the minimization language.

If any of the five standard exclusions are absent, flag it.

### IV. Obligations & Restrictions

- **Standard of care**: "Reasonable care" or "same degree of care as own CI, but no less than reasonable care" are **both market-standard** — do not flag these. "Best efforts" or "strictest confidence" impose a higher standard — flag only if materially different from reasonable care. **Note:** Aspirational language like "strict confidence" paired with a "reasonable care" floor is standard NDA drafting — the phrases serve complementary purposes (aspiration + minimum standard) and should not be flagged as creating tension or ambiguity.
- **Permitted disclosures**: Employees, contractors, advisors, and professional consultants is **standard**. Affiliates are common but not required — their absence alone is not an issue. Must recipients be bound by comparable obligations? That requirement is standard and appropriate.
- **Non-compete / non-solicitation**: These do **not** belong in NDAs. A non-compete appears in roughly 5% of NDAs and is almost always challenged. Non-solicitation appears in roughly 12% — it should be in a separate agreement if needed. Flag prominently but at Medium severity unless the scope is unreasonably broad.
- **Reverse engineering prohibition**: Relevant in tech/IP contexts. Its **presence is not inherently problematic** — do not flag standard reverse engineering prohibitions as issues.
- **Return/destruction obligations**: Return or destroy CI upon termination is standard. Must include exception for legal/regulatory retention and automated backup systems — absence of this exception is operationally impossible to comply with. **Certification of destruction is reasonable** — do not flag it. Sworn affidavit is onerous. Check for asymmetry: does only one party have return obligations?
- **Warranty disclaimer**: Check whether warranty disclaimers are mutual. A one-sided disclaimer (only one party's CI is provided "as-is") means the other party may be impliedly warranting the accuracy of its information.

### V. Term, Duration & Survival

- **Agreement term**: 1-3 years is standard. Auto-renewal is common and acceptable.
- **Survival period**: 2-3 years post-termination is typical for general CI. 5 years is on the long end but **within market range** — note it as a minor observation. Perpetual survival is aggressive but acceptable for trade secrets with an appropriate carve-out. Perpetual for ALL information is a red flag.
- **Asymmetric survival**: If the survival period differs between the parties in a mutual NDA, this is a significant issue.
- **Termination rights**: Either party should be able to terminate on reasonable notice (30 days is standard). One-sided termination rights or asymmetric notice periods are a red flag.
- **Trade secret carve-out**: Trade secrets should remain protected for as long as they qualify under applicable law, separate from the general survival period. This is appropriate and standard.

### VI. Remedies & Liability

- **Injunctive relief**: Acknowledgment of irreparable harm and right to seek equitable relief is **standard**. Must be mutual in mutual NDAs. **Bond waiver**: even when mutual, a bond waiver removes a procedural safeguard — note it as a minor observation since it is not uniformly enforceable across jurisdictions.
- **Indemnification**: Almost always inappropriate in NDAs. The receiving party should not indemnify for breach beyond standard contract damages. If present, check scope and symmetry carefully.
- **Consequential damages**: Read the actual language carefully. A straightforward mutual exclusion of consequential damages is worth noting because in NDA breaches, the primary harms (lost business, competitive harm, reputational damage) are consequential. But also watch for **circular or self-negating structures** — provisions where each party appears to have coverage but provisos or exceptions strip it away. Parse the sentence structure to determine what each party can actually recover.
- **Limitation of liability / damages cap**: Note the cap amount and whether it's reasonable relative to the potential harm. Check whether indemnification or specific categories are carved out from the cap.
- **Liquidated damages**: Unusual in NDAs. Flag if present.
- **Attorney's fees**: One-way fee-shifting (only if discloser wins) is aggressive. Each side pays own fees is standard in the US absent a contractual provision.

### VII. Boilerplate (That Isn't Really Boilerplate)

- **Governing law & jurisdiction**: Note the jurisdiction. Mandatory venue in the counterparty's home state can be expensive. **Both parties in the same state makes this a non-issue** — note the forum selection but do not flag it as problematic.
- **Dispute resolution**: Litigation is standard. Arbitration can be appropriate for confidentiality disputes (inherently private). Neither is inherently problematic.
- **Assignment**: Standard: no assignment without consent, with a carve-out for M&A (assignee assumes obligations). **This is market-standard** — do not flag it. Watch for low control thresholds (e.g., 20% ownership) that could route CI to competitors through affiliate chains. Standard threshold is 50%+.
- **Entire agreement / integration**: Should supersede prior discussions on this subject matter. Only flag if it **explicitly voids prior confidentiality agreements entirely** — that could eliminate protections under stricter earlier NDAs. Standard integration clauses are not an issue.
- **No obligation to proceed**: Standard disclaimer that neither party is obligated to enter into a further business relationship.
- **Notice provisions**: Standard notice clauses (written notice, overnight courier, registered mail) are market-standard. **Do not flag the absence of email as a notice method** — this is a drafting choice, not a deficiency.
- **Provisions NOT expected in standard NDAs**: Do not flag the absence of data security/privacy obligations, MNPI/no-trading language, specific technology protections, or damages caps. These belong in separate agreements or are not standard NDA terms. An NDA's absence of provisions that belong elsewhere is not an issue.

## Step 3: Cross-Provision Synthesis

After reviewing individual clauses, step back and assess the agreement as a whole:

- **Pattern recognition**: Is this a balanced agreement, or is it systematically one-sided despite appearing mutual? Connect individual findings into a pattern — three or more provisions favoring the same party suggests intentional drafting, not oversight.
- **Provision interactions**: Do individually reasonable provisions create exploitable gaps when combined? Consider: purpose definition + residuals clause (broad purpose + residuals = licensed competitive use); assignment + affiliate definitions (low thresholds can route CI to competitors); indemnification + damages exclusions (mutual indemnification minus consequential damages may be hollow for the discloser).
- **Most/least favorable interpretation**: For any ambiguous provision, consider what the counterparty could argue it permits under the most aggressive reading. This is where subtle drafting (like "wrongful act" vs. "fault") matters.
- **Context calibration**: How does the business context amplify or mitigate what you found? A PE firm getting access to pharma pipeline data amplifies every gap. Two peer companies exploring a routine integration mitigates most minor issues.

## Step 4: Calibration Check

Before writing your output, review your findings against these criteria. This step separates expert analysis from over-lawyering.

**For each issue you identified, ask:**
- Would a senior partner at a top firm flag this to the client, given the specific business context? Or would they consider it standard and move on?
- Is this a genuine deviation from market practice, or just a provision that could theoretically be better?
- Does the business context (party roles, information sensitivity, power dynamic, competitive landscape) make this provision actually risky, or is the risk theoretical?

**Important: calibration cuts both ways.**
- If a provision uses standard language and applies symmetrically, do not flag it as an issue. Standard provisions are not issues.
- But if a "mutual" NDA applies different rules to each party — different definitions, different marking requirements, different disclosure permissions, different survival periods, different return obligations — that asymmetry IS the issue, even if each individual provision uses standard-looking language. Systematic one-sidedness disguised as mutual language is precisely what this review should catch.
- If the business context amplifies risk (PE firm + trade secrets, competitor + core IP, large company + startup), flag provisions that would be acceptable between peer companies in a routine deal.

**Remove false positives** — standard provisions that you incorrectly flagged as issues. But **keep genuine observations** at appropriate severity — a thorough review includes minor notes (Low severity) alongside major issues. Err on the side of noting things the client should be aware of, even if they wouldn't negotiate over them.

**For the overall recommendation, apply these thresholds strictly:**
- **SIGN**: The agreement is within market range and obligations are genuinely symmetric. This includes agreements with Low-severity observations AND Medium-severity items that this methodology describes as "within market range," "minor observation," "note it," or "acceptable" — these inform the client but do not block signing. A well-drafted, balanced NDA between peer companies should be recognized as signable even if imperfect. When in doubt: if a senior partner would tell the client "this is fine, sign it and move on," recommend SIGN.
- **NEGOTIATE**: The agreement has Medium or High severity issues that represent **genuine deviations from market standard creating real risk** — not just provisions that are on the long end of market range or theoretically improvable. There must be specific changes you would ask for that a reasonable counterparty would understand. Finding issues does not automatically mean NEGOTIATE; the issues must be ones a senior partner would actually hold up signing over.
- **DON'T SIGN**: The agreement has **fundamental structural problems that cannot be fixed with targeted redlines** — systematic one-sidedness (3+ provisions favoring the same party in ways that create exploitable gaps), missing critical exclusions, embedded restrictive covenants that change the nature of the agreement, or the document is not actually an NDA. This is rare. If each issue you found can be addressed with a specific, reasonable redline, the correct recommendation is NEGOTIATE, not DON'T SIGN — even if there are many issues.

## Step 5: Output

### Executive Summary
- NDA type, parties, and the client's position
- Overall risk classification: **GREEN** (sign or sign with minor comments), **YELLOW** (negotiate specific terms), or **RED** (reject, substantially redraft, or counter with own form)
- 2-3 sentence explanation calibrated to context

**GREEN**: The NDA is within market range. Obligations are symmetric (or appropriately asymmetric for a unilateral NDA), all standard exclusions are present, no non-compete/non-solicitation/scope creep, reasonable term and survival, standard remedies. Minor imperfections and market-range observations do not prevent execution. Most well-drafted mutual NDAs between peer companies are GREEN.

**YELLOW**: One or more provisions create **real, actionable risk** that a senior partner would hold up signing over. Not merely "on the long end of market range" — genuinely problematic provisions that require specific changes. Provisions may individually seem reasonable but collectively create exploitable gaps.

**RED**: Fundamental issues — wrong NDA type, missing critical exclusions, embedded restrictive covenants, systematic one-sidedness, unreasonable terms, or the document is not actually an NDA. The problems cannot be fixed with a short redline list. This is rare.

### Issues

| Section | Issue | Severity | Specific Risk | Suggested Redline |
|---------|-------|:--------:|---------------|-------------------|
| § ___ | Brief issue title | H/M/L | What could go wrong in practice | Recommended change |

- **H (High)**: Material risk — creates uncapped exposure, operationally impossible obligations, or fundamentally unfair terms
- **M (Medium)**: Real risk that should be negotiated but is manageable
- **L (Low)**: Below market standard or worth noting, but does not materially change risk profile

Only include genuine issues. Standard, market-reasonable provisions should not appear in this table.

### Top 3 Negotiation Priorities
If recommending NEGOTIATE: the three most important changes, in priority order. For each: what to ask for (preferred) and what to accept (fallback).

### Overall Recommendation
- **SIGN**: Acceptable as-is. State what makes it standard.
- **NEGOTIATE**: Sign after specific changes. List required changes.
- **DON'T SIGN**: Reject. Explain why and recommend next step (counter with own form, walk away, or escalate).
